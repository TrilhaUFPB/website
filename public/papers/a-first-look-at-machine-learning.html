A first look at Machine Learning

How spam filters, scatter plots, and a stubborn taxi driver explain the field — without a single line of math.

MQ
by Malu Quintela
18 min read · based on Géron, Ch. 1

In 1959, an IBM engineer named Arthur Samuel taught a computer to play checkers — not by writing down every rule of the game, but by letting the machine play thousands of games against itself and slowly figure out which moves tended to win. The program got better than he was. He called what it did machine learning, and the name stuck. Today the same idea reads your email and decides which messages are spam. This article walks through what that means, where it works, and where it quietly goes wrong.

§ 01What is Machine Learning?

Machine learning is the discipline of getting a computer to improve at a task by showing it examples instead of writing the rules by hand. That's the whole idea. Two famous definitions sharpen it a little.

Machine Learning is the field of study that gives computers the ability to learn without being explicitly programmed. — Arthur Samuel, 1959

A few decades later, Tom Mitchell offered a tidier, more engineering-flavored version: a program is said to learn from experience E with respect to some task T and performance measure P, if its performance on T, as measured by P, improves with E. It sounds clinical, but the pieces are easy to spot in any concrete example. Take a spam filter. The task is sorting emails into "spam" or "not spam." The experience is a collection of past emails labeled by humans — what's called a training set, with each labeled example a training instance. The performance measure might be accuracy: the fraction of new emails the system labels correctly. If accuracy climbs as we feed it more examples, the system is learning.

That's the vocabulary. Now for the part that's actually interesting: why we'd want to do this in the first place.

§ 02Why use Machine Learning?

Imagine you've been asked to write a spam filter the old-fashioned way. You sit down and study a few hundred spam messages. You notice certain words and phrases — "4U", "credit card", "free", "amazing" — that show up over and over. So you write a rule: if an email contains any of these, flag it as spam. You miss some, so you add more rules. Some legitimate emails get caught, so you add exceptions. After a few weeks you have a brittle pile of if-statements that nobody, including you, fully understands.

Now consider the machine-learning approach. Instead of writing rules, you collect a pile of emails — some labeled spam, some labeled ham (legit) — and let an algorithm figure out which patterns separate one from the other. The algorithm does the rule-making. You do the data-collecting. The resulting filter is usually shorter, more accurate, and easier to maintain.

The advantage shows up most clearly when the world changes. Suppose spammers notice your filter blocks anything containing "4U". They start writing "For U" instead. With hand-written rules, somebody has to spot this, log in, and add a new clause. With a machine-learning filter, you just feed in the new examples and the system updates on its own.

§ 03Types of Machine Learning systems

"Machine learning" is a big tent. To make sense of what's inside, people sort systems along three independent axes. First, by how much human supervision the training requires — this gives us supervised, unsupervised, semisupervised, and reinforcement learning. Second, by when the learning happens — all at once on a fixed dataset (batch) or continually as new data arrives (online). And third, by how the system makes predictions — by comparing new inputs to memorized examples (instance-based) or by building a predictive model from those examples (model-based).

A real system usually has a label from each axis. A spam filter, for instance, might be supervised, online, and model-based. We'll spend the rest of this article on the first and third axes, since they're the ones beginners run into immediately.

§ 04Supervised, unsupervised, and friends

Supervised learning

In supervised learning, every example you show the algorithm comes with the answer attached. Spam filters again: each email in the training set has been tagged "spam" or "ham" by a human. The algorithm's job is to learn the relationship between the email's contents and that label, so that when a new, unlabeled email arrives, it can guess the label correctly. This kind of task — predicting a category — is called classification.

Supervised algorithms also do regression, which is the same idea except the answer is a number instead of a category. Predicting the price of a used car from its mileage, age, and brand is a regression problem. Instead of "spam vs. ham," the output is a continuous value.

The toolbox is well-stocked: k-Nearest Neighbors, linear and logistic regression, support vector machines, decision trees and random forests, and, of course, neural networks.

Unsupervised learning

Sometimes nobody has labeled anything. You just have a heap of data and a hunch that there's structure in it. That's the setting for unsupervised learning. The algorithm gets the inputs but not the answers, and it tries to find pattern, group, or simplification on its own.

The most common unsupervised task is clustering — putting similar items in the same group. A music app might cluster listeners by what they play; suddenly there's a group that listens to lo-fi at night and metal in the morning, and you didn't have to define that audience in advance. Other unsupervised tasks include dimensionality reduction (simplifying data by squeezing it into fewer dimensions while preserving structure), anomaly detection (flagging credit-card transactions or sensor readings that don't fit the usual pattern), and association rule learning (the supermarket classic: people who buy diapers also buy beer).

Semisupervised learning

In practice, labels are expensive — somebody has to sit down and tag things — while raw data is cheap. Semisupervised learning mixes a small pile of labeled examples with a much larger pile of unlabeled ones. Google Photos is the textbook example. Upload a thousand pictures and the app first clusters all the faces it sees (unsupervised). You tag two or three: "this is Alice." Now it knows what those clusters mean, and it can label the rest. Tiny human effort, big payoff.

Reinforcement learning

Reinforcement learning is the strangest member of the family, and the most fun to watch. There's no training set and no labels. There's an agent that acts in some environment — a robot in a room, a player in a game — and after each action it gets a numerical reward (or penalty). The agent's job is to figure out, by trial and error, a policy: a strategy for choosing actions that adds up to the most reward over time. This is how DeepMind's AlphaGo learned to play Go better than any human; it's how robots learn to walk; it's how recommendation systems learn which suggestion you'll click.

§ 05Instance-based vs. model-based

There's a second, deeper way to split learning systems: by what the algorithm actually keeps around after training. Two answers, two philosophies.

Learning by heart

The simplest possible thing a "learner" can do is just memorize all the training examples. When a new instance arrives, it compares the new one to everything it has seen before and picks the closest matches. This is instance-based learning. The spam filter version: flag a new email as spam if it has a lot of words in common with emails already labeled spam. There's no abstraction. The training set is the model.

Building a model

The other approach is to extract a general pattern from the training data and throw most of the data away. You keep the pattern. This is model-based learning. Suppose we have data from a few dozen countries: each country's GDP per capita on one axis, and the average self-reported life satisfaction of its residents on the other. We notice that, roughly, richer countries report higher satisfaction. We could draw a straight line through the cloud that captures this trend. The line is the model. It has just two numbers — a slope and an intercept — and once we've picked them, we can plug in any GDP and read off a predicted satisfaction.

The catch, of course, is choosing the slope and intercept well. You want the line to be close to as many points as possible. "Close" needs a definition, so we pick a number that gets bigger when the line is bad — usually the sum of squared distances between each point and the line — and ask the algorithm to find the slope and intercept that make that number as small as possible. That's what training a linear model means, behind the curtain. The same general idea — define a model, define a measure of badness, search for parameters that minimize the badness — powers everything from linear regression up to enormous neural networks.

§ 06When models learn the wrong lesson: overfitting

Here is a story. A tourist hails a taxi in a new city. The driver overcharges them, drives in circles, and leaves them at the wrong hotel. The tourist concludes that all taxi drivers in this city are crooks. This is overgeneralization, and it's something both humans and machines do, frequently.

In machine learning, this is called overfitting: the model performs beautifully on its training data and falls apart on anything new. The mechanism is exactly the same as the tourist's. Show a flexible enough model a small set of examples and it will find some pattern that explains every single one of them — including the noise.

The classic visual is fitting a high-degree polynomial through a handful of points. The curve passes through every training point exactly, but between and beyond those points it does wild things.

What do you do about overfitting? You constrain the model — force it to be simpler than the data alone would let it be. This is called regularization, and you can think of it as turning down a dial: the higher the dial, the less the model is allowed to wiggle. A good regularization setting forces the model to ignore quirks of the training set and focus on the broader trend. The number that controls how strongly you regularize isn't a parameter the algorithm learns from data — it's a knob you set before training begins. Such knobs have a special name: hyperparameters. Finding the right values for them is most of the work of building a real model.

In a sentence

Overfitting is when a model memorizes the noise instead of the signal. Regularization is the act of giving the model less room to memorize.

§ 07The opposite problem: underfitting

If overfitting is the model learning too much from too little, underfitting is the model failing to learn enough. Imagine trying to fit a straight line through a clearly curved set of points — say, daily temperature over a year. No matter how you tilt the line, it can't capture the seasonal arc. The model is simply not powerful enough for the structure in the data. Its training error is high and its predictions are bad — both signs that something has gone wrong, but a different kind of wrong than overfitting.

There are three common fixes. You can switch to a more powerful model with more parameters and more flexibility — a curve instead of a line, or a deeper network. You can feed the model better features — extra columns of derived information that help expose the structure (in the temperature example, maybe "day of year" isn't enough; maybe "sine of day of year" is). Or you can dial down the regularization, if you turned it too far up and constrained the model into uselessness.

Underfit ↔ Overfit

Picture a slider that runs from "too rigid" on the left to "too flexible" on the right. The art is finding the sweet spot. Lean too far left and the model can't capture what's there; too far right and it captures things that aren't.

§ 08Stepping back

We've covered a lot. Here's the field on a single page.

What ML is. A way of getting computers to improve at a task by showing them examples, instead of by hand-writing rules. We measure that improvement with a number — accuracy, error, reward — and call the examples a training set.

The main flavors. Supervised systems learn from labeled examples (classification and regression). Unsupervised systems find structure in unlabeled data (clustering, dimensionality reduction, anomaly detection). Semisupervised systems get a small label budget and stretch it across mostly-unlabeled data. Reinforcement learning agents discover good behavior by acting and receiving rewards. Cutting in a different direction, learners are either instance-based (memorize and compare) or model-based (build a compact predictor).

What a typical ML project looks like. You gather and prepare data. You pick a model — linear, tree, neural network, whatever fits the shape of the task. You train it: the algorithm searches for parameters that minimize some measure of error on the training data. You evaluate it on data it hasn't seen, to find out whether it actually generalizes. And then you ship it, and it goes wrong in interesting ways, and you collect more data and start again.

The two ways things go wrong. Underfitting (the model is too simple for the patterns in the data) and overfitting (the model latches onto patterns that aren't really there). Between them sits the dial you're really turning when you build an ML system: how much structure to let the model find, and how strictly to constrain it.

What data hides before Machine Learning

An introduction to Exploratory Data Analysis and the questions worth asking before you train anything at all.

BC
by Beatriz Cruz
20 min read · 7 sections

§ 01The Problem

why looking at the data matters

Imagine training a machine learning model with perfect accuracy, only to realize later that your dataset had duplicated users, missing values, impossible ages, and a correlation that existed only because the data was collected during a holiday campaign.

That is not a rare failure. It is one of the most common ways machine learning projects become impressive demos and disappointing products.

Modern teams are obsessed with models: bigger architectures, cleaner APIs, better metrics, faster inference. But the model is usually not where the first truth lives. The first truth is in the rows, the columns, the missing cells, the weird spikes, and the boring-looking averages that hide two completely different groups of people.

Garbage in, garbage out is not a slogan. It is a data pipeline with consequences.

Exploratory Data Analysis, or EDA, is the discipline of slowing down before prediction. It asks: what is actually here? What is broken? What is repeated? What is suspiciously convenient? What human behavior produced this shape?

§ 02What is EDA?

exploring before predicting

John Tukey helped popularize Exploratory Data Analysis as a way of approaching data with curiosity before formal modeling. The idea is simple and still radical: before you test a hypothesis, sometimes you need to discover which hypotheses are worth having.

EDA is less about graphs and more about curiosity. A histogram is not the point. A scatter plot is not the point. The point is learning to ask better questions after every view of the data.

A good analyst looks at a chart and does not immediately say "the answer is X." They say, "this could be X, or it could be Y, and here is what I would check next."

the EDA mindset

Every chart answers one question and raises three new ones. The job is not to find the answer — it is to learn which questions are worth asking next.

§ 03Distributions

most data is not evenly distributed

Real data rarely arrives as a neat bell curve. Purchase times cluster around lunch breaks and late nights. Online activity spikes after notifications. Ticket values often have a long tail: many small purchases, a few huge ones.

If you only look at the average, you compress all of that behavior into one number. That number may be mathematically correct and analytically useless.

Distributions show the shape of behavior. They reveal concentration, skewness, outliers, and subgroups. They help you notice whether "the typical user" exists, or whether you invented them by averaging unlike people together.

§ 04Correlations

when patterns lie

Correlation is seductive because it gives shape to coincidence. Two lines rise together and the brain wants a story. Sometimes the story is real. Sometimes both variables are responding to a third thing you forgot to measure.

The classic example is ice cream sales and drowning incidents. They can rise together, but ice cream does not cause drowning. Hot weather increases both swimming and ice cream demand.

This is why EDA needs skepticism. A pattern is not a conclusion. It is an invitation to investigate mechanism, bias, measurement, and context.

A pattern is not a conclusion. It is an invitation to investigate.

§ 05Human behavior hidden in data

people leave patterns everywhere

This is where EDA becomes more than a technical checklist. Most datasets are shadows of human behavior. People buy after payday. They browse late at night. They return to products they trust. They abandon forms that feel too long. They behave differently when they are new, tired, rushed, loyal, or confused.

Rows can look mechanical, but they often come from habits. Recurrence, seasonality, hesitation, bursts, and silence all leave traces.

The analyst's job is not to romanticize the data. It is to remember that behind many events there was a person, a context, and a reason.

§ 06EDA before Machine Learning

why models fail before training

Many model failures are born before the first epoch, before the first split, before the first metric. The dataset already contains the failure.

Imagine a team building a model to predict whether a user will buy this week. The dataset includes browsing behavior, account age, email clicks, and one suspiciously powerful column: coupon_used. During training, that column predicts purchases almost perfectly.

The problem is timing. A coupon can only be used during or after checkout. If the goal is to predict before checkout, that feature is information from the future. The model did not learn customer intent; it learned a shortcut that will not exist at prediction time.

Three failure modes EDA can catch

Leakage is the cleanest example of a model failing before training. The metric is high, the demo is convincing, and the system is unusable because the training data contained a clue that production will never have.

Overfitting can start the same way: if the dataset is tiny, repetitive, or collected during one unusual campaign, the model learns that moment instead of the broader behavior.

Bias appears when some people, contexts, or outcomes are missing from the data. The model learns the world it was shown, not the world it will meet.

EDA catches these issues by asking when each column becomes known, who is missing, what period the data describes, and whether a suspiciously good signal is actually valid.

✓
before training, check

Missingness · duplicates · target leakage · temporal splits · class balance · outliers · collection process · whether your metric reflects the decision the model will actually support.

§ 07Conclusion

learning to read before teaching machines

EDA is not the glamorous part of machine learning, but it is often the part that decides whether the project deserves a model at all.

It turns raw data into questions. It turns charts into hypotheses. It turns suspicious accuracy into a warning. It turns human behavior into something visible enough to reason about.

The best analysts do not treat EDA as a box to check before the real work begins. They treat it as the first real work.

Before teaching machines how to learn from data, we must first learn how to read it ourselves.
